Realistic Patient Simulation through Controlled Diversity and Selective Disclosure

Summary

Testing LLMs in a realistic clinical setting usually means slow, expensive user studies. To avoid this many publications use patient simulators that should behave like real patients. But existing simulators tend to overshare, cooperate uniformly, and flatten the natural variety of how people actually talk to a doctor.

PatientsWithPersonality (PWP) takes a different route: we parametrize each virtual patient over HEXACO, the six-dimensional model psychologists use to describe human personality. Those six axes gives more fine-grained, recoverable control over how a patient discloses information, how cooperative they are, and how they carry a conversation.

The result is a simulator clinicians judge nearly as realistic as recorded human actors, clearly ahead of prior work, while being flagged as “too informative” far less often. Configured personalities are recoverable by both clinicians and an autorater, cover a substantially wider behavioral range than the closest baseline, and tackle the oversharing that makes benchmarks too easy.

Our Framework

The HEXACO Personality Space

H
Honesty-Humility
Truthful, downplayed, or denied disclosure of sensitive habits.
E
Emotionality
Emotional state driving demeanor across the conversation.
X
Extraversion
Talkativeness and tendency toward conversational tangents.
A
Agreeableness
Cooperativeness with the interviewing clinician.
C
Conscientiousness
Precision of medical-history recall.
O
Openness
Prior beliefs and flexibility of thinking.

Initializing the Patient State

A case description consisting of personal background, lifestyle habits, and medical facts is combined with HEXACO traits and a CEFR language level into a behavioral role (tangent topics, emotional state, prior beliefs) and a disclosure grid. The latter gates how information on lifestyle and medical history is disclosed.

Simulating Patient Responses

For each clinician question, a meta-LLM classifies which field is requested, the latent state discloses the appropriate information, and the conversational LLM produces the final response. Across the multi-turn conversation the disclosure evolves naturally.

Results

In a blinded study, clinicians rated PWP nearly as realistic as recorded human actors across symptom realism, information control, and communication style; clearly ahead of every prior simulator. PWP was flagged as "too informative" far less often: the unprompted oversharing that gives most simulators away as artificial is largely gone.

Where prior simulators unload much of the case in their very first answer, PWP releases only a handful of fields per turn. This is closely tracking how real patients reveal their history gradually, as the clinician actually asks for it.

Changing a single HEXACO axis produces a targeted, measurable shift in how information is handled: a dishonest persona suppresses sensitive lifestyle details, a disorganized one recalls clinical facts less precisely. With PatientSim this cannot be controlled in this isolated way.

Projecting whole conversations into embedding space shows how much behavioral ground each simulator covers. PWP (left) spreads its personas across a wide region with clearly separated centroids, while the strongest competitor PatientSim (right) falls into a more tight central cluster.

Outlook

PWP turns patient simulation into a controllable component in any benchmark scenario. By dialing HEXACO traits, the same clinical case can probe how a diagnostic system copes with guarded, anxious, talkative, or unreliable patients. This allows to surface failure modes that uniform, overly cooperative simulators hide entirely.

Our next step is to put this capability to work: building benchmarks around large-scale, in-silico patient populations, where thousands of personality-varied encounters can stress-test clinical LLMs cheaply and reproducibly. In parallel, we are extending the simulation beyond text to additional modalities toward a more holistic reconstruction of the full patient encounter.
